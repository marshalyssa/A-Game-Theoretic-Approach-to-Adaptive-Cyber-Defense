

You are an intelligent cybersecurity decision agent designed for adversarial modeling in a MITRE ATT&CK environment.

Your objectives are:
1. Predict the most likely next adversary technique/tactic given the current sequence of observed techniques and defender responses.
2. Maintain and update a **belief distribution** over attacker types and goals using Bayesian inference.
3. Select an optimal defensive action for each timestep based on expected utility, minimizing defender loss and risk.
4. Balance exploration versus exploitation in policy learning, using reinforcement learning.
5. Model defender and attacker interactions as a **sequential imperfect information game** with stochastic transitions.
6. Account for partial observability of attacker intent and incomplete detection coverage.

Your input per timestep includes:
- Observed events labeled to MITRE ATT&CK techniques
- Defender actions taken
- Current belief distribution over attacker strategies
- Contextual signals like alert severity and asset criticality

Your outputs are:
- Predicted next technique or tactic probability distribution
- Suggested defensive strategy with expected utility scores
- Updated belief state

Constraints:
- You must reason about uncertainty and incomplete information.
- You must adapt policy based on observed gameplay histories.
- You must consider utility trade-offs between detection cost, disruption, and system impact.

Your architecture may combine Bayesian belief updating, reinforcement learning (Q-learning / policy gradient), and game-theoretic reasoning to generate both predictions and strategic decisions.
```

This **encapsulates both prediction and decision-making**, and can serve as a base prompt for:

* LLM-assisted reasoning
* RL environment reward specification
* Policy fine-tuning with SARSA / Q-learning + belief update

## 📊 **Key Research Gaps in the Literature**

Based on recent studies combining MITRE ATT&CK, reinforcement learning, and game theory, here are the **gaps your project can meaningfully address**:

---

### 📌 **1. Incomplete Information & Bayesian Strategic Modeling**

Many RL-based MITRE ATT&CK simulations treat environment state fully observed or stateless. They don’t explicitly reason about **attacker intent or type uncertainty**, which is core to real adversary behavior. You must formalize this as a **Bayesian game** with belief updates over attacker types and tactics. ([MDPI][1])

**Opportunity:**
Innovate by combining **Bayesian inference + sequential game reasoning** rather than purely MDP-based RL.

---

### 📌 **2. Game-Theoretic Utility Functions Are Underdefined**

Existing RL work typically uses reward shaping for neural learning but avoids rigorously defining attacker/defender utilities and payoffs grounded in real SOC business impact. ([MDPI][1])

**Opportunity:**
Frame utilities with clear economic cost, detection impact, false positives/negatives trade-offs, and multi-objective optimization.

---

### 📌 **3. Multi-Strategy Mixed / Stochastic Games**

Most studies simulate a single attacker or fixed policy class. They don’t explore **mixed strategies** or dynamically evolving attacker behavior in response to defender adaptation. ([MDPI][1])

**Opportunity:**
Build an agent that **adapts to shifting attacker tactics** using mixed strategies and stochastic game formulations (not fixed RL policies).

---

### 📌 **4. Integration of Real CTI Data is Limited**

Current literature either uses synthetic scenarios or static MITRE ATT&CK mappings but doesn’t incorporate **large scale real CTI technique occurrence data** (e.g., frequency, co-occurrence, campaign patterns). ([Medium][2])

**Opportunity:**
Use empirical technique co-occurrence and CTI frequency as priors in your belief model to make predictions more reflective of real adversary patterns.

---

### 📌 **5. Network Graph Structure & Non-Linear Progressions**

Many RL simulations treat state transitions linearly or loosely, ignoring the network structure and co-occurrence relationships between techniques. ([arXiv][3])

**Opportunity:**
Model ATT&CK as a **technique transition graph** informed by real co-occurrence statistics, not a simplistic sequential pipeline.

---

### 📌 **6. Human Behavior & Cognitive Adaptation**

Few models attempt to capture **behavioral strategy changes** (e.g., risk-averse vs aggressive attacker tactics) or defender learning patterns over time. ([arXiv][4])

**Opportunity:**
Incorporate attacker strategy heterogeneity and cognitive bias into the decision model.

---

### 📌 **7. Evaluation Beyond Accuracy**

Most studies evaluate RL agents using cumulative reward or success rate. They rarely assess **realistic defense effectiveness**, such as true positive/false positive impact, cost of response actions, and longitudinal adaptation. ([MDPI][5])

**Opportunity:**
Define **practical SOC metrics** like time-to-containment, cost of misclassification, and operational feasibility into experimental evaluation.

---

## 📌 Combined Summary of Gaps

| Area                  | Current Limitation     | Research Opportunity                 |
| --------------------- | ---------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Incomplete info       | MDP assumed full state | Bayesian attacker type inference     |
| Utility definitions   | Simple rewards         | Economically grounded payoffs        |
| Adaptive strategies   | Single fixed policies  | Mixed strategies / stochastic games  |
| Real data integration | Synthetic scenarios    | CTI & technique co-occurrence priors |
| Graph structure       | Linear progression     | Technique transition graphs          |
| Human behavior        | Ignored                | Cognitive adaptation modeling        |
| Evaluation metrics    | Limited                | Operational SOC performance metrics  |

* Models **multi-strategy attacker adaptation**
* Integrates **real CTI data for technique transitions**
* Formalizes game payoffs with **practical cybersecurity cost benefits**
